Cat food questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Dry cat food offers convenience and shelf stability, but grain-free diets are only necessary for cats with specific allergies. Look for named animal protein as the first ingredient and a statement from AAFCO indicating nutritional adequacy. No single formula is best for every cat; your choice should align with your cat’s age, health, and preferences.

What are the benefits and drawbacks of dry cat food?

Dry cat food, also called kibble, is popular because it’s convenient: it doesn’t spoil quickly, can be left out for free-feeding, and is often easier on a household budget than canned food. Many cats enjoy the crunchy texture, and the mechanical action of chewing may help with dental health, though it’s not a substitute for regular dental care. The main drawbacks are its low moisture content, typically around 6–10%. which can be a concern for cats prone to urinary tract issues or dehydration. Kibble also tends to be more calorie-dense, so overeating can lead to obesity. Some dry foods contain more carbohydrates than a cat’s natural diet would include, though that is not inherently harmful for healthy cats.

Is grain-free cat food better for my cat?

Grain-free formulas replace grains like corn, wheat, or rice with alternative carbohydrate sources such as potatoes, peas, or lentils. For most cats, grains are not a problem; true grain allergies are very rare. A grain-free diet is only beneficial if your cat has a confirmed intolerance or allergy to specific grains. Recent veterinary research has linked certain grain-free diets to a heart condition called dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), though the exact cause is still being studied, it may be related to low taurine or to ingredients like legumes and pulses. For an average healthy cat, a diet that includes grains is perfectly acceptable and often more affordable. Always consult your vet before making a drastic diet change.

What should I look for in a dry cat food ingredient list?

Start with the first ingredient: it should be a named animal source like “chicken,” “salmon meal,” or “deboned turkey.” Cats are obligate carnivores and need meat-based protein. Avoid vague terms like “meat meal” or “poultry by-product” if you prefer transparent sourcing, though by-products can be nutritious. Look for the presence of taurine (an essential amino acid) and omega fatty acids. A short list of recognizable ingredients is often a good sign, but the most critical factor is that the food meets AAFCO nutritional guidelines. Avoid foods with excessive artificial colors, preservatives, or vague fillers like “corn gluten meal” if your cat is sensitive.

How do I know if a cat food meets nutritional standards?

Check the package for a statement that says the food is “formulated to meet AAFCO Cat Food Nutrient Profiles” or “complete and balanced.” This means the recipe provides all necessary nutrients in the proper ratios for a specific life stage, such as growth, maintenance, or all life stages. Without this statement, the food may be intended only as a treat or supplement. Feeding trials, where the food is tested on real cats, are even more rigorous but are not required for every brand. Some premium brands voluntarily conduct feeding trials and will mention it on the label or website. For kittens or cats with medical conditions, look for the appropriate life-stage designation to ensure proper nutrition.

How much dry food should I feed my cat?

Feeding amounts vary widely by brand, recipe, and your cat’s weight, age, and activity level. Start with the feeding guide on the bag, which usually gives a range per weight. For example, a typical guideline might be around 1/3 to 1/2 cup per day for a 10-pound cat, but these are estimates. Obesity is very common in cats, so measure portions accurately using a kitchen scale rather than a cup. Many vets recommend splitting the daily portion into two or more meals. Adjust the amount based on your cat’s body condition: you should be able to feel ribs easily without them protruding. If you also feed wet food, reduce the dry portion accordingly to avoid overfeeding.

Can dry food cause health problems like urinary issues or obesity?

Dry food’s low moisture content can contribute to chronic dehydration in cats who don’t drink enough water, which is a risk factor for urinary crystals, bladder stones, and kidney disease. To mitigate this, encourage water intake with multiple bowls, a pet fountain, or by adding water to the kibble. Some cats on dry-only diets also develop obesity if portions aren’t controlled. That said, many cats thrive on high-quality dry food for their whole lives. The key is choosing a nutritionally complete kibble, feeding the right amount, and providing fresh water. If your cat has a history of urinary issues, a wet food diet or a prescription urinary diet is often recommended by veterinarians.

Frequently asked questions

Is grain-free cat food necessary for cats with allergies?

Not usually. Most food allergies in cats are caused by animal proteins like chicken, beef, or fish, not grains. A grain-free diet is only helpful if a grain intolerance has been confirmed by a veterinarian through an elimination diet.

Are by-products in cat food bad?

By-products are organ meats and other parts not typically eaten by humans. They can be highly nutritious and are a natural part of a cat’s ancestral diet. The quality depends on the source, reputable brands use clean, inspected by-products. Avoid if the ingredient list does not specify the animal source.

Should I feed my cat a combination of wet and dry food?

A mixed diet offers the best of both worlds: the moisture and high protein of wet food plus the dental benefits and convenience of dry food. Many owners find that feeding wet food in the morning and leaving dry out during the day works well. Just adjust total calories to prevent overfeeding.

How do I transition my cat to a new food?

Gradually increase the new food over 7–10 days to avoid digestive upset. Start with 25% new food mixed with 75% old, then shift to 50/50, then 75/25, and finally 100% new. If your cat starts vomiting or has diarrhea, slow down the transition or consult your vet.

What does ‘complete and balanced’ mean on cat food labels?

It means the food contains all the essential nutrients in the right amounts for a specific life stage (growth, maintenance, or all life stages) as defined by AAFCO. This is the minimum standard for a food to be your cat’s sole nutrition source.

Is it safe to feed my cat a dry food only diet?

Yes, for healthy cats who drink enough water, a high-quality dry food that is complete and balanced can be perfectly safe. However, cats on dry-only diets have a higher risk of dehydration and urinary issues, so monitor water intake and consider adding moisture to the kibble.

In shortChoosing a cat food is about matching the food to your individual cat’s needs rather than chasing trends. A high-quality dry kibble with real meat, appropriate nutrients, and a clear AAFCO statement can be a solid foundation. For most cats, grain-inclusive formulas are just as good as grain-free, and a mix of wet and dry food offers the most balanced approach. Always check with your veterinarian before making significant dietary changes.
