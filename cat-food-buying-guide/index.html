How to choose the right cat food

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

The best cat food for your pet depends on their age, health needs, and preferences, not on trendy labels like grain-free. Focus on high-quality protein sources, a named meat as the first ingredient, and AAFCO nutritional adequacy statements. Grain-free isn’t automatically healthier; it’s only useful if your cat has a diagnosed grain allergy or sensitivity.

What should I look for in a dry cat food label?

First, flip the bag over and scan the guaranteed analysis and ingredient list. The first ingredient should be a named animal protein (chicken, salmon, turkey). not a meal or a vague by-product. Look for a statement from the Association of American Feed Control Officials (AAFCO) that the food is ‘complete and balanced’ for your cat’s life stage. Next, check the crude protein and fat levels. Adult cats generally need around 30-40% protein on a dry-matter basis. Avoid foods that rely heavily on corn, wheat, or soy as fillers; they offer minimal nutritional value. Remember that ‘natural’ is an unregulated term, it doesn’t guarantee quality.

Is grain-free cat food better for my cat?

Not necessarily. Grain-free diets replace grains like rice or corn with alternative carbohydrates such as potatoes, peas, or lentils. For most cats, grains are perfectly digestible and provide energy and fiber. The real benefit of grain-free food often comes from higher protein and lower carb formulations, not the absence of grains themselves. However, a small number of cats do have true grain allergies, usually showing up as itchy skin or digestive upset. If your cat shows these signs, a grain-free diet may help. For everyone else, there’s no proven advantage. In fact, some grain-free foods have been linked to a rare heart condition called dilated cardiomyopathy (DCM), though the connection is still being studied. When in doubt, stick with a well-known brand that follows AAFCO guidelines and has a history of safety.

How do I choose between dry and wet cat food?

Dry food is convenient, shelf-stable, and can help with dental health, though the crunching effect is modest. It also tends to be more affordable per feeding. Wet food, on the other hand, provides much-needed moisture for cats, who are naturally low drinkers. That extra water is a big help in preventing urinary tract issues and kidney disease. Most veterinarians recommend feeding a combination: some high-quality dry kibble for convenience and dental texture, plus canned food to boost hydration. If your budget is tight, prioritize a good dry food and make sure your cat always has fresh water. Cats that eat only dry food should be encouraged to drink more, perhaps with a pet fountain.

What are the most important nutrients for cats?

Cats are obligate carnivores, which means they need nutrients found only in animal tissue. The top non-negotiables are taurine (an amino acid vital for heart and eye health), arachidonic acid (a fatty acid), and preformed vitamin A. Without these, cats can develop serious health problems. Also look for a good balance of protein and fat, with limited carbohydrates. Cats have no dietary need for carbs, though small amounts help with kibble structure. Omega-3 fatty acids (from fish oil or flaxseed) are a nice bonus for skin and coat health. Avoid foods with excessive phosphorus for senior cats or those with kidney issues.

How much should I feed my cat each day?

Feeding guidelines on the bag are just that, guidelines. Your cat’s ideal amount depends on their weight, age, activity level, and whether they also eat treats or wet food. A typical adult cat needs about 1/4 to 1/2 cup of dry food per day, split into two meals. Use a measuring cup, not a scoop, to be precise. Start with the lower end of the recommendation and adjust based on your cat’s body condition. You should be able to feel their ribs without pressing hard, and see a distinct waist when looking from above. If they’re gaining weight, cut back slightly. If they’re losing weight or always hungry, increase portion size or switch to a higher-calorie food. For mixed feeding (dry + wet), you’ll need to reduce the dry amount to avoid overfeeding.

Frequently asked questions

Can I feed my cat only dry food?

Yes, many cats thrive on a high-quality dry food as long as they drink enough water. To reduce the risk of dehydration and urinary issues, provide a clean fountain and monitor water intake. If your cat has a history of urinary crystals or kidney disease, consider adding some wet food.

What does ‘crude protein’ mean on the label?

Crude protein is a chemical estimate of the total protein content, not a measure of quality. A high percentage is good, but you also want to know the source. Named animal proteins (chicken meal, salmon) are more digestible than plant proteins (corn gluten, soybean meal).

Are by-products bad for cats?

Not inherently. By-products can include organ meats like liver, which are very nutritious for cats. The problem is when by-products come from unnamed sources (e.g., ‘meat by-products’) or low-quality rendering. A specific by-product like ‘chicken liver’ is fine.

How do I transition my cat to a new food?

Gradually over 7 to 10 days. Start with 75% old food and 25% new, then shift ratios every couple of days. A sudden change can cause vomiting or diarrhea. If your cat is a picky eater, you can warm the new food slightly to release aroma, or mix in a little of the old food’s juices.

Is human-grade cat food worth the cost?

Human-grade means it meets USDA standards for human consumption, involving stricter processing and quality checks. It’s generally higher quality but significantly more expensive. For most cats, a premium pet food from a reputable brand (Purina Pro Plan, Hill’s Science Diet, Royal Canin) provides complete nutrition without the premium price tag.

In shortChoosing the right cat food doesn’t have to be overwhelming. Stick with a reputable brand that clearly states AAFCO nutritional adequacy, has a named animal protein as the first ingredient, and matches your cat’s life stage and health. Skip the marketing hype, grain-free isn’t a magic bullet, and always consult your veterinarian before making big diet changes, especially if your cat has a medical condition.
